Revolution   of   Grocery   Shop
The grocery business is changing and it’s changing fast. You can call it a revolution, another victim in the retail apocalypse, the latest sector to be disrupted by tech or yet another industry being “killed” by millennials. One thing is for certain: a grocery revolution began in earnest in 2017, and it’s far from over. An industry that was the stable, boring bedrock of the consumer market for decades came under pressure from all directions this past year. The moats which had protected this reliable business were drained by new technologies, growing consumer revolt from our current unsustainable food system, and a looming logistics revolution on the horizon.



Food had always been local, and this new system changed that. It centralized vendor purchasing and in the process eliminated a lot of the transparency. Food is a basic human need. It is virtually recession proof, with thin margins offset by scale. Consumers are loyal to their brands, and relatively consistent in their buying habits. Much (but not enough) of the food we eat is perishable and requires semi-regular restocking trips. It makes for a dull, but steady business, with a few large multinationals controlling most of the market.



There was a revolution in the grocery business in 2017, and it’s far from over. The industry will further resemble a dumbbell, with the lowest cost, most convenient providers on one end, and the most talented curators of products and experiences on the other and not much left in the middle. Remember A&P? They filed for Chapter 11 in 2015. Just as the rise of the grocers made the current food system possible at the turn of the 20th century, all these influences in the beginning of the 21st century are going to create a new food system. There will be more of what worked, enabled by technology to increase scale, efficiency, and logistics, as well as an opportunity for something new, healthier, and more sustainable. The outcome will be determined by those companies responsive enough to listen and adjust to the changing tastes and demands of consumers.
